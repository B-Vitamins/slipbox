*** Second-Order Linear ODEs
**** Singular points
Consider the linear homogeneous second-order ODE

\begin{align*}
D_{x}^{2} y + P(x) D_{x} y + Q(x) y = 0.
\end{align*}

+ Points $x_{0}$ for which $P(x)$ and $Q(x)$ are finite are termed *ordinary points* of the ODE.
+ Point $x_{0}$ (finite) for which $\lim_{x \to x_{0}} P(x) = \pm \infty$ or $\lim_{x \to x_{0}} Q(x) = \pm \infty$ are called *singular points*
+ A singular point $x_{0}$ (finite) is *regular* if $\lim_{x \to x_{0}} \left \lvert \left( x - x_{0} \right) P(x) \right \rvert < \infty$ (given $\lim_{x \to x_{0}} P(x) = \pm \infty$) or $\lim_{x \to x_{0}} \left \lvert \left( x - x_{0} \right)^{2} Q(x)  \right \rvert < \infty$ (given $\lim_{x \to x_{0}} Q(x) = \pm \infty$).
+ A singular point $x_{0}$ (finite) is *irregular* if $\lim_{x \to x_{0}} \left( x - x_{0} \right) P(x) = \pm \infty$ (given $\lim_{x \to x_{0}} P(x) = \pm \infty$) or $\lim_{x \to x_{0}} \left( x - x_{0} \right)^{2} Q(x)  = \pm \infty$ (given $\lim_{x \to x_{0}} Q(x) = \pm \infty$).
+ For $x_{0} = \pm \infty$, make the substitution $x = 1/z$, and examine the ODE's behaviour for $z \to 0$.

#+NAME: Hypergeometric ODE
\begin{align*}
x \left( x - 1 \right) D_{x}^{2} y + \left[ \left( 1 + a + b \right) x + c \right] D_{x} y + ab y = 0.
\end{align*}

This is the *Hypergeometric ODE*. It has regular singularities at $x =0, 1, \infty$ and no irregular singularities.

#+NAME: Legendre ODE
\begin{align*}
\left( 1 - x^{2} \right) D_{x}^{2} y - 2 x D_{x} y + l \left( l + 1 \right) y = 0.
\end{align*}

This is the *Legendre ODE*. It has regular singularities at $x =-1, 1, \infty$ and no irregular singularities.

#+NAME: Chebyshev ODE
\begin{align*}
\left( 1 - x^{2} \right) D_{x}^{2} y - x D_{x} y + n^{2} y = 0.
\end{align*}

This is the *Chebyshev ODE*. It has regular singularities at $x =-1, 1, \infty$ and no irregular singularities.

#+NAME: Confluent Hypergeometric ODE
\begin{align*}
x D_{x}^{2} y + \left( c - x \right) D_{x} y - a y = 0.
\end{align*}

This is the *Confluent Hypergeometric ODE*. It has a regular singularity at $x = 0$ and a irregular singularity at $x =\infty$.

#+NAME: Bessel ODE
\begin{align*}
x^{2} D_{x}^{2} y + x D_{x} y + \left( x^{2} - n^{2} \right) y = 0.
\end{align*}

This is the *Bessel ODE*. It has a regular singularity at $x = 0$ and a irregular singularity at $x =\infty$.

#+NAME: Laguerre ODE
\begin{align*}
x D_{x}^{2} y + \left( 1 - x \right) D_{x} y + a y = 0.
\end{align*}

This is the *Laguerre ODE*. It has a regular singularity at $x = 0$ and a irregular singularity at $x =\infty$.

#+NAME: Simple Harmonic Oscillator
\begin{align*}
D_{x}^{2} y + \omega^{2} y = 0.
\end{align*}

This is the *Simple Harmonic Oscillator ODE*. It has a no regular singularity and a irregular singularity at $x =\infty$.

#+NAME: Hermite ODE
\begin{align*}
D_{x}^{2} - 2 x D_{x} y + 2 \alpha y = 0.
\end{align*}

This is the *Hermite ODE*. It has a no regular singularity and a irregular singularity at $x =\infty$.
**** Frobenius's method
Consider the linear homogeneous second-order ODE

\begin{align*}
D_{x}^{2} y + P(x) D_{x} y + Q(x) y = 0.
\end{align*}

There exist only two independent solutions for this ODE. The *most general solution* is

\begin{align*}
y \left( x \right) = c_{1} y_{1} \left( x \right) + c_{2} y_{2} \left( x \right),
\end{align*}

where $y_{1}(x)$ and $y_{2}(x)$ are independent solutions. Consider the linear homogeneous second-order ODE

\begin{align*}
D_{x}^{2} y + P(x) D_{x} y + Q(x) y = F \left( x \right).
\end{align*}

Assuming a single *particular integral* $y_{p}(x)$ of the inhomogeneous ODE to be available, it may be added to any solution of the corresponding homogenous equation so that the *most general solution* is

\begin{align*}
y \left( x \right) = c_{1} y_{1} \left( x \right) + c_{2} y_{2} \left( x \right) + y_{p} \left( x \right).
\end{align*}

where $y_{1}(x)$ and $y_{2}(x)$ are independent solution of the corresponding homogenous ODE.

The constants $c_{1}$ and $c_{2}$ will be fixed by boundary conditions.

Frobenius's method consists of proposing an ansatz of the form:

\begin{align*}
\tilde{y} \left( x \right) = (x - x_{0})^{s} \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} a_{n} \left( x - x_{0} \right)^{n}, \qquad a_{0} \neq 0,
\end{align*}

and feeding it into the differential equation.

If $x_{0}$ is an ordinary point or at worst a regular singular point, the Frobenius method will yield /at least/ one solution (Fuch's theorem).

1. If the two roots of the indicial equation are equal, we can obtain only one solution by the series substitution method.
2. If the two roots differ by a nonintegral number, two independent solutions may be obtained.
3. If the two roots differ by an integer, the larger of the two will yield a solution, while the smaller may or may not give a solution, depending on the behaviour of the coefficients.

Care must be taken to ensure that $x_{0}$ is /not/ a irregular singular point for the ODE under consideration, for then even a single solution is not guaranteed. Assuming $x_{0}$ is /not/ an irregular singular point:

+ From the uniqueness of power series, the total coefficient of each power of $x$ must vanish.
+ The requirement that the /first/ coefficient vanish leads to the *indicial equation*. It has two roots.
+ Depending on the root being considered, the /second/ coefficient must either /necessarily/ vanish or be arbitrary. So we just /demand/ that it be $0$ and save ourselves the trouble of cross-referencing with the root of the indicial equation.
+ Imposing the vanishment of the coefficients of $x^{n+s}$ furnishes a *recurrence relation* which assigns the coefficients $\left \lbrace a_n  \right \rbrace$.
+ The acceptability of a series solution is contigent not only on it satisfying the differential equation but only on its convergence (including asymptotic convergence) over the region of interest. One does /not/ imply the other.
**** Symmetry of solutions

Writing a general homogeneous ODE as

\begin{align*}
\mathcal{L} \left( x \right) y \left( x \right) = 0,
\end{align*}

in which $\mathcal{L} \left( x \right)$ is the differential operator, we have:

\begin{align*}
\mathcal{L} \left( x \right) = \mathcal{L} \left( -x \right), \quad \mathcal{L} \left( x \right) y \left( x \right) = 0 \quad  \Longrightarrow \mathcal{L} \left( x \right) y \left( - x \right) = 0,
\end{align*}

\begin{align*}
\mathcal{L} \left( x \right) = - \mathcal{L} \left( -x \right), \quad \mathcal{L} \left( x \right) y \left( x \right) = 0 \quad  \Longrightarrow - \mathcal{L} \left( x \right) y \left( - x \right) = 0.
\end{align*}

The solutions of ODEs involving differential operators with a certain parity are either even or odd, or they are linearly independent solutions that can be combined into a pair of solutions, one even, and one odd, by forming

\begin{align*}
y_{\text{even}} = y \left( x \right) + y \left( -x \right), \quad y_{\text{odd}} = y \left( x \right) - y \left( -x \right).
\end{align*}

The differential operators for the /linear oscillator/, /Legendre/, /Chebyshev/, /Bessel/, /simple harmonic oscillator/ and /Hermite equations/ are all based on differential operators with even parity; i.e.

\begin{align*}
P \left( x \right) = - P \left( x \right), \quad Q \left( x \right) = Q \left( -x \right),
\end{align*}

so solutions of all of them may be presented as series of even powers of $x$ or separate series of odd powers of $x$. The /Laguerre/ differential operator has neither even nor odd symmetry; hence its solutions cannot be expected to exhibit even or odd parity.
**** Linear independence of solutions
Consider a set of functions $\left \lbrace \varphi_{\lambda} \left( x \right) \right \rbrace$. The criterion for *linear dependence* is

\begin{align*}
\sum_{\lambda} k_{\lambda} \varphi_{\lambda} \left( x \right) = 0.
\end{align*}

in which not all the coefficients $k_{\lambda} = 0$. If satisfied, the set $\left \lbrace \varphi_{\lambda} \left( x \right) \right \rbrace$ forms a vectors space where the elements are functions or a *function space*.

We have

\begin{align*}
S = \left \langle \sum_{\lambda} k_{\lambda} \varphi_{\lambda} \Bigg \vert \sum_{\mu} k_{\mu} \varphi_{\mu} \right \rangle \Longrightarrow \sum_{\lambda} k_{\lambda} \varphi_{\lambda} \left( x \right) \neq 0.
\end{align*}

/Assume/ that all members of $\left \lbrace \varphi_{\lambda} \left( x \right) \right \rbrace$ are infinitely differentiable. Differentiate it as many times as the cardinality of the set $\left \lbrace \varphi_{\lambda} \left( x \right) \right \rbrace$, say $n$, form the determinant

\begin{align*}
\begin{vmatrix}
\varphi_{1} & \varphi_{2} & \dotso & \varphi_{n} \\
\varphi_{1}^{\prime} & \varphi_{2}^{\prime} & \dotso & \varphi_{n}^{\prime} \\
\dotso & \dotso & \dotso & \dotso \\
\varphi_{1}^{n-1} & \varphi_{2}^{n-1} & \dotso & \varphi_{n}^{n-1} \\
\end{vmatrix} \equiv W \left( x \right)
\end{align*}


This determinant is called the *Wronskian*. We have

\begin{align*}
\sum_{\lambda} k_{\lambda} \varphi_{\lambda} \left( x \right) = 0 \Longrightarrow W \left( x \right) = 0.
\end{align*}

If $W \left( x \right) \neq 0$ /for all/ $x$, then the set of functions $\left \lbrace \varphi_{\lambda} \left( x \right) \right \rbrace$ are *linearly independent*.

Using the Wronskian, it is straightforward to prove that a linear second-order homogeneous ODE has at most two linearly independent solutions. Generalizing, a linear homogeneous $n-$th order ODE has at most $n$ linearly independent solutions $y_{j}$, and its general solution will be of the form $y \left( x \right) = \sum_{j=1}^{n} c_{j} y_{j} \left( x \right)$.
**** Second solution using the Wronskian
Consider the linear homogeneous second-order ODE

\begin{align*}
D_{x}^{2} y + P(x) D_{x} y + Q(x) y = 0.
\end{align*}

Let $y_{1}$ and $y_{2}$ be two independent solutions. Then the Wronskian, by definition, is

\begin{align*}
W = y_{1} y_{2}^{\prime} - y_{1}^{\prime} y_{2} = y_{1}^{2} D_{x} \left( \dfrac{y_{2}}{y_{1}} \right).
\end{align*}

and we have

\begin{align*}
D_{x} W = - P \left( x \right) W,
\end{align*}

which has solution

\begin{align*}
W \left( x \right) = W \left( a \right) \exp \left \lbrace - \int_{a}^{x} P \left( x_{1} \right) d x_{1} \right \rbrace.
\end{align*}

We also have

\begin{align*}
D_{x} \left( \dfrac{y_{2}}{y_{1}} \right) = W \left( a \right) \dfrac{\exp \left \lbrace - \int_{a}^{x} P \left( x_{1} \right) d x_1  \right \rbrace}{y_{1}^{2}},
\end{align*}

\begin{align*}

* Sturm-Liouville Theory
** Notes
*Sturm-Liouville theory* deals with eigenvalue problems of the form

\begin{align*}
\mathcal{L} \psi \left( x \right) = \lambda \psi \left( x \right), \quad \mathcal{L} \left( x \right) = p_{0} \left( x \right) D_{x}^{2} + p_{1} \left( x \right) D_{x} + p_{2} \left( x \right).
\end{align*}

$\mathcal{L} \left( x \right)$ is *self-adjoint* if

\begin{align*}
D_{x} p_{0} \left( x \right) = p_{1} \left( x \right).
\end{align*}

We have

\begin{align*}
D_{x} p_{0} \left( x \right) = p_{1} \left( x \right) \Longrightarrow \mathcal{L} \left( x \right) = D_{x} \left[ p_{0} \left( x \right) D_{x} \right] + p_{2} \left( x \right).
\end{align*}

so that the Sturm-Liouville problem becomes

\begin{align*}
D_{x} \left[ p_{0} \left( x \right) D_{x} \psi \left( x \right) \right] + \left( p_{2} \left( x \right) - \lambda \right)  \psi \left( x \right) = 0.
\end{align*}

We are interested in an integral of the form $\int_{a}^{b} v^{\ast} \left( x \right) \mathcal{L} u \left( x \right) dx$. Why this interest? Well, why not? If you must know, it reduces to an important mathematical object called the *scalar product* under certain conditions.

\begin{align*}
\int_{a}^{b} v^{\ast} \left(\mathcal{L} u \right) dx &= \int_{a}^{b} \left[ v^{\ast} D_{x} \left[ p_{0} D_{x} u \right] + v^{\ast} p_{2} u \right] \thinspace dx \\
&= \left[ v^{\ast} p_{0} D_{x} u \right]_{a}^{b} + \int_{a}^{b} \left[ - \left( D_{x} v^{\ast} \right) p_{0} D_{x} u + v^{\ast} p_{2} u \right] \thinspace dx \\
&= \left[ v^{\ast} p_{0} D_{x}u - \left( D_{x} v^{\ast} \right) p_{0} u \right]_{a}^{b} + \int_{a}^{b} \left[ D_{x} \left[ p_{0} D_{x} v^{\ast} \right] u + v^{\ast} p_{2} u \right] \thinspace dx \\
&= \left[ v^{\ast} p_{0} D_{x} u - D_{x} v^{\ast} p_{0} u \right]_{a}^{b} + \int_{a}^{b} \left( \mathcal{L} v \right)^{\ast} u dx.
\end{align*}

When $\left[ v^{\ast} p_{0} D_{x} u - D_{x} v^{\ast} p_{0} u \right]_{a}^{b}$ vanishes,

\begin{align*}
\int_{a}^{b} v^{\ast} \left(\mathcal{L} u \right) dx = \int_{a}^{b} \left( \mathcal{L} v \right)^{\ast} u dx.
\end{align*}

It may vanish in various ways:

1) If $u(x=a) = u (x = b) = 0$ or if $v^{\ast} \left( x = a \right) = v^{\ast} \left( x = b \right) = 0$, the surface term vanishes. The problem is then said to have *Dirchlet boundary conditions*.
2) If $D_{x} u \left( b \right) = D_{x} u \left( a \right) = 0$ or $D_{x} v^{\prime} \left( b \right) = D_{x} v^{\ast} \left( a \right) = 0$, the surface term vanishes. The problem is then said to have *Neumann boundary conditions*.
3) If $v^{\ast} (b) D_{x} u (b) = (D_{x} v^{\ast} (a)) u (a)$, the surface term vanishes. The problem is then said to have *Mixed/Robin boundary conditions.* This is usually the case in periodic systems, like a crystal lattice.

If $u$ and $v$ are eigenfunctions of $\mathcal{L}$ with respective /real/ eigenvalues $\lambda_{u}$ and $\lambda_{v}$, the equation reduces to

\begin{align*}
\left( \lambda_{u} - \lambda_{v} \right) \int_{a}^{b} v^{\ast} u dx = \left[ p_{0} \left[ v^{\ast} D_{x} u - \left( D_{x} v^{\ast} \right) u \right] \right]_{a}^{b}.
\end{align*}

If the boundary term vanishes and $\lambda_{u} \neq \lambda_{v}$, then $u$ and $v$ must be orthogonal on the interval $(a,b)$. This is a specific illustration of the orthogonality requirement for eigenfunctions of a Hermitian operator in a Hilbert space.

A Sturm-Liouville problem of the form

\begin{align*}
\mathcal{L} \psi \left( x \right) = \lambda \psi \left( x \right), \quad \mathcal{L} \left( x \right) = p_{0} \left( x \right) D_{x}^{2} + p_{1} \left( x \right) D_{x} + p_{2} \left( x \right).
\end{align*}

in which $\mathcal{L}$ is /not/ self-adjoint can be /made/ self-adjoint by multiplying throughout with

\begin{align*}
w \left( x \right) = p_{0}^{-1} \exp \left \lbrace \int \dfrac{p_{1} \left( x \right)}{p_{0} \left( x \right)} dx  \right \rbrace.
\end{align*}

$w \left( x \right) \mathcal{L} \left( x \right)$ will then satisfy the self-adjoint condition for we will have:

\begin{align*}
w \left( x \right) \mathcal{L} \left( x \right) = \bar{p_{0}} D_{x}^{2} + \bar{p_{1}} D_{x} + w \left( x \right) p_{2} \left( x \right),
\end{align*}

where

\begin{align*}
\bar{p_{0}} = \exp \left \lbrace \int \dfrac{p_{1} \left( x \right)}{p_{0} \left( x \right)} dx  \right \rbrace, \quad \bar{p_{1}} = \dfrac{p_{1}}{p_{0}} \exp \left \lbrace \int \dfrac{p_{1} \left( x \right)}{p_{0} \left( x \right)} dx  \right \rbrace.
\end{align*}

We then have:

\begin{align*}
\int_{a}^{b} v^{\ast} w \left( x \right) \mathcal{L} u \left( x \right) dx = \left[ v^{\ast} \bar{p_{0}} D_{x} u - \left( D_{x} v^{\ast} \right) \bar{p_{0}} u \right]_{a}^{b} + \int_{a}^{b} w (x) \left( \mathcal{L} v \right)^{\ast} u dx.
\end{align*}

When the scalar product is defined to be

\begin{align*}
\left \langle v \vert u \right \rangle = \int_{a}^{b} v^{\ast} \left( x \right) u \left( x \right) w \left( x \right) dx
\end{align*}

and if the boundary term vanishes, then this equation in Dirac notation reduces to

\begin{align*}
\left \langle v \left \lvert \mathcal{L}  \right \rvert u \right \rangle = \left \langle \mathcal{L} v \vert u \right \rangle.
\end{align*}

If $u$ and $v$ are eigenfunctions of $\mathcal{L}$, with respective eigenvalues $\lambda_{u}$ and $\lambda_{v}$, we have

\begin{align*}
\left( \lambda_{u} - \lambda_{v} \right) \int_{a}^{b} v^{\ast} u w dx = \left[ w p_{0} \left[ v^{\ast} D_{x} u - \left( D_{x} v^{\ast} \right) u \right] \right]_{a}^{b}.
\end{align*}

If the boundary term vanishes and $\lambda_{u} \neq \lambda_{v}$, then $u$ and $v$ must be orthogonal on the interval $(a,b)$ under a scalar product defined with the weight factor $w$.

In summary:

1. If a second-order differential operator $\mathcal{L}$ has coefficients $p_{0}(x)$ and $p_{1}(x)$ that satisfy the self-adjoint condition $D_{x} p_{0} \left( x \right) = p_{1} \left( x \right)$, then it is *Hermitian* given:
   1. a scalar product of uniform weight,
   2. boundary conditions that remove the surface term $\left[ v^{\ast} p_{0} D_{x} u - D_{x} v^{\ast} p_{0} u \right]_{a}^{b}$.
2. If $D_{x} p_{0} \left( x \right) = p_{1} \left( x \right)$ is not satisfied by the differential operator $\mathcal{L}$, then it is *Hermitian* given:
   1. a scalar product is defined to include the weight factor $w \left( x \right) = p_{0}^{-1} \exp \left \lbrace \int \dfrac{p_{1} \left( x \right)}{p_{0} \left( x \right)} dx  \right \rbrace$.
   2. boundary conditions remove the surface term $\left[ w p_{0} \left[ v^{\ast} D_{x} u - \left( D_{x} v^{\ast} \right) u \right] \right]_{a}^{b}$.

Once the problem has been defined such that $\mathcal{L}$ is Hermitian, then the general properties proved for Hermitian problems apply: the eigenvalues are real; the eigenfunctions are (or if degenerate can be made) orthogonal, *using the relevant scalar product definition.*

Any self-adjoint differential operator satisfying the above conditions is Hermitian, but a Hermitian operator *may not* be self-adjoint.

\end{equation*}

\end{equation*}

*If there is some common region in which $w_{1} = u(x, y) + i v(x, y)$ and* $w_{2} = w^{*}_{1} = u(x, y) - i v(x, y)$ *are both analytic, prove that $u(x, y)$ and $v(x, y)$ are constants.* \\

u_{x}& =- v_{y},\\
u_{y}& =- v_{x}&
u_{y}& =- \left(- v_{x} \right) = v_{x}.
\end{align*}

*Show that given the Cauchy-Riemann equation, the derivative $f'(z)$ has the same value for $dz = a dx + i b dy$ (with neither $a$ nor $b$ zero) as it has for $dz = dx$.* \\

First we show that if $f(x, y)$ is analytic in some domain, then $f'(z) = \frac{\partial f}{\partial x} = \frac{\partial f}{i \partial y}$. We begin by considering the differential $df$

\[
df = \frac{\partial f}{\partial x} dx + \frac{\partial f}{\partial (i y)} d (i y).
\]

Because $d (i y) = i dy$ and $\frac{\partial}{\partial (i y)} = \frac{\partial}{i \partial y}$

\begin{align*}
df &= \frac{\partial f}{\partial x} dx + \frac{\partial f}{\partial y} dy\\
&= (u_{x} + i v_{x}) dx + (u_{y} + i v_{y}) dy.
\end{align*}

Because, by appeal to CRE, $(u_{x} + i v_{x}) = (- i u_{y} + v_{y})$,

\[
df = (u_{x} + i v_{x}) (dx + i dy) = \frac{\partial f}{\partial x} dz,
\]

and 

\[
df = (- i u_{y} + v_{y}) (dx + i dy) = \frac{\partial f}{i \partial y} dz,
\]

it follows that

\[
\frac{df}{dz} = \frac{\partial f}{\partial x} = \frac{\partial f}{i \partial y}. \quad \blacksquare
\]

Assume that $a$ and $b$ are /not/ functions of $x$ and $y$. The differential $df$, given a change $dz = a dx + i b dy$, $a \neq 0$, $b \neq 0$ in $z$ is

\[
df = \frac{\partial f}{\partial (a x)} d(a x) + \frac{\partial f}{\partial (i b y)} d(i b y).
\]

Because

\[
\frac{\partial f}{\partial (a x)} =\frac{\partial f}{a \partial x},
\]

\[
\frac{\partial f}{\partial (i b y)}& =\frac{\partial f}{i b \partial y},
\]

$d(a x) = a dx$, and $d(i b y) = i b dy$, the differential $df$ of the function $f$ becomes

\begin{align*}
df &= \frac{\partial f}{a \partial x} (a dx) + i \frac{\partial f}{i b \partial y} (b dy) \\
&= \frac{\partial f}{\partial x} dx + \frac{\partial f}{\partial y} dy\\
&= \frac{\partial f}{\partial x} (dx + i dy)\\
&= \frac{\partial f}{\partial x} dz.
\end{align*}

It follows that

\[
f'(z) = \frac{\partial f}{\partial x}
\]

for $dz = a dx + i b dy$. $\blacksquare$

The differential $df$, given a change $dz = dx$ is

\[
df = \frac{\partial f}{\partial x} dx = \frac{\partial f}{\partial x} dz.
\]

It follows

\[
f'(z) = \frac{\partial f}{\partial x},
\]

for $dz = dx$. $\blacksquare$

Therefore, the derivative $f'(z)$ has the same value for $dz = a dx + i b dy$ (with neither $a$ nor $b$ zero) as it has for $dz = dx$. $\blacksquare$

\end{equation*}

$p_{-1} = 1, \thinspace p_{i} = 0, i \neq -1$, $q_{-2} = - \nu^{2}, \quad q_{0} = 1, \quad q_{j} = 0, j \neq -2, 0$ so that $s^{2} + (p_{-1} - 1)s + q_{-2} = 0$ reduces to

\begin{equation*}
s^{2} - \nu^{2} = 0,
\end{equation*}

which is in fact the /indicial equation/ and has solutions

\begin{equation*}
s = \pm \nu.
\end{equation*}

We have used Frobenius's method (see =7.6.24=). The ansatz for the larger root is of the form

\begin{align*}
y(x) = x^{s} \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} c_{n} x^{n} \xrightarrow[]{s = \nu} x^{\nu} \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} c_{n} x^{n},
\end{align*}

which on feeding to the ODE, re-labelling indices and demanding vanishing coefficients furnishes the recurrence:

\begin{equation*}
c_{n} = \dfrac{c_{n-2} + c_{n} [(n + s) - \nu^{2}] }{\left( n + s \right)\left( n + s - 1 \right)},
\end{equation*}

which we juggle a bit

\begin{equation*}
c_{n} \left[ (n + s) - \nu^{2} + \left( n + s \right) \left( n + s - 1 \right)\right] = c_{n} \left[ (n + s) - \nu^{2} + \left( n + s \right)^{2} - \left( n + s \right) \right] = c_{n} \left[ (n + s)^{2} - \nu^{2} \right] =  c_{n-2},
\end{equation*}

and reduce to

\begin{equation*}
c_{n} = -\dfrac{c_{n-2}}{ \left( n + s \right)^{2} - \nu^{2}}.
\end{equation*}

If $\nu$ is not an integer, then the two recurrences yield two linearly independent solutions which are infact the Bessel functions of the first kind $J_{\pm \nu}(x)$ of order $\pm \nu$. If $\nu$ is an /positive/ integer, then for the smaller root $s = - \nu$, $c_{n}$ diverges when $n = 2 \nu$. Thus the first term of $y_{2}$ must necessarily be proportional to $x^{- \nu} x^{2 \nu} = x^{\nu}$, same as the first term of $y_{1}$. To proceed we set up the Wronskian double integral (see =7.6.23=):

\begin{align*}
y_{2} \equiv Y_{\nu} \left( x \right) = J_{\nu} \left( x \right) \int^{x} \dfrac{\exp \left \lbrace - \int^{x^{2}} P \left( x_{1} \right) d x_{1} \right \rbrace}{ \left[ J_{\nu} \left( x_{2} \right) \right]^{2}} d x_{2}.
\end{align*}

On substitute $P \left( x_{1} \right) = x^{-1}$ and integrating we get:

\begin{align*}
Y_{\nu} \left( x \right) = J_{\nu} \left( x \right) \ln \left \lvert x  \right \rvert + x^{-\nu}\sum_{n=0}^{\infty} b_{n} x^{n},
\end{align*}

which defines the Bessel function of second kind upto a multiplicative factor and an additive term. The coefficients $b_{n}$ are determined by feeding $Y_{\nu} \left( x \right)$ into the Bessel equation and demanding vanishment of the coeffcients of the resulting series.
