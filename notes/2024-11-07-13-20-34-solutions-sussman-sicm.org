*Fermat observed that the laws of reflection and refraction could be accounted for by the following facts: Light travels in a straight line in any particular medium with a velocity that depends upon the medium. The path taken by a ray from a source to a destination through any sequence of media is a path of least total time, compared to neighboring paths. Show that these facts imply the laws of reflection and refraction.*

Imagine two fixed points separated by a distance $b$. One of the fixed points is the source of light, other the destination, light bouncing off a line parallel to the line segment formed by the fixed points and at a distance $a$ from it. Where is incidence? A positive truth value of Fermat's principle is equivalent to demanding that the point of incidence minimize the perimeter of the triangle formed by it, the source and the destination. Travelling in a direction opposite that the destination is a no-brainer so we'll discard that right off the bat. Our starting point then is a right angle triangle, the right angle stationed at the source. Suppose $a^{2} + b^{2} \equiv c^{2}$. As the supposed to be point of incidence slides a distance $x$ along the line past the source, the sides of the triangle transform as

\begin{equation*}
a \to \sqrt{a^{2} + x^{2}}, \quad c \to \sqrt{a^{2} + \left( b - x \right)^{2}},
\end{equation*}

so that the perimeter $P$ as a function of $x$ is

\begin{equation*}
P(x) = \sqrt{a^{2} + x^{2}} + \sqrt{a^{2} + \left( b - x \right)^{2}},
\end{equation*}

upto an additive constant. Point of incidence is obtained by solving

\begin{equation*}
D P (x) = \dfrac{2x}{\sqrt{a^{2} + x^{2}}} - \dfrac{2 (b - x) }{\sqrt{a^{2} + \left( b-x \right)^{2}}} = 0
\end{equation*}

for $x$. Rearrange and square to obtain

\begin{equation*}
\dfrac{x^{2}}{a^{2} + x^{2}} = \dfrac{(b-x)^{2}}{a^{2} + \left( b - x \right)^{2}} \Longrightarrow x^{2} = \left( b - x \right)^{2},
\end{equation*}

which yields $x = b/2$, an isosceles triangle. Law of reflection must obviously hold.

Imagine now the basically the same scenario but bend the line so that it intersects the line segment formed by the source and the destination at some arbitrary angle. Also, light now cruises through instead of bouncing back. Might as well swirl it around till the line coincides with the $x$ axis.

Light could of course head straight for the destination (where it would, in general, be travelling along the diagonal of some rectangle say with sides $b$ and $a + c$, $b$ being the length of the sides parallel to the $x$ axis, $a + b$ being the length of the side perpendicular ot the $x$ axis, $a$ and $c$ being the lengths of the side perpendicular ot the $x$ axis below and above the $x$ axis respectively) but what else could it do?

If the $x$ axis were the boundary separating two media so that light cruises at speed $v_{1}$ beyond and $v_{2}$ within, it could, with due reverence to Sir Fermat, do a cost-benefit analysis of reaching the $x$ axis at some angle $\theta_{1}$ (and whatever $\theta_{2}$ that dictates - it /will/ dictate a $\theta_{2}$ because while detours are permitted, changing the destination is not), find out if the return on investment breaks even. For given $v_{1}$, $v_{2}$, a point sliding on the $x$ axis generates possible values of $\theta_{1}$ and $\theta_{2}$, and the consequent market returns. Of course, letting the point slide out of the rectangle defined above would be dumb, even if it were only "light"-hearted. Let $x$ be the amount by which this point slides from one of the rectangle's sides to within the rectangle. The travel time $T(x)$ as a function of $x$  is

\begin{equation*}
T(x) = \dfrac{\sqrt{x^{2} + a^2}}{v_{1}} + \dfrac{\sqrt{c^{2} + \left( b - x \right)^{2}}}{v_{2}},
\end{equation*}

which light will minimize cause at its heart, it just wants to be fast, or so says Fermat, so solve

\begin{equation*}
D T(x) = \dfrac{2x}{v_{1} \sqrt{a^{2}+x^{2}}} - \dfrac{2 (b - x)}{v_{2} \sqrt{c^{2} + \left( b - x \right)^{2}}} = 0,
\end{equation*}

for $x$. Actually just identify

\begin{equation*}
\dfrac{x}{\sqrt{a^{2} + x^{2}}} = \sin \theta_{1}, \quad \dfrac{b-x}{\sqrt{c^{2} + \left( b - x \right)^{2}}} = \sin \theta_{2}
\end{equation*}

to eliminate $x$ and obtain the rewrite

\begin{equation*}
\dfrac{\sin \theta_{1}}{v_{1}} - \dfrac{\sin \theta_{2}}{v_{2}} = 0
\end{equation*}

which is a step shy of the /law of refraction/

\begin{equation*}
\dfrac{\sin \theta_{1}}{\sin \theta_{2}} = \dfrac{v_{1}}{v_{2}}.
\end{equation*}

*Suppose that x is the constant-velocity straight-line path of a free particle, such that* $x_{a} = x(t_{a})$ *and* $x_{b} = x(t_{b})$. *Show that the action on the solution path is*

\begin{equation*}
\dfrac{m}{2} \dfrac{\left( x_{b} - x_{a} \right)^{2}}{t_{b} - t_{a}}.
\end{equation*}

We know (how?)

\begin{equation*}
S \left[ q \right](t_{1}, t_{2}) = \int_{t_{1}}^{t_{2}} L \circ \Gamma \left[ q \right].
\end{equation*}

For the free particle Lagrangian and the constant-velocity straight-line path

\begin{equation*}
L \left( t, x, v \right) = \dfrac{1}{2} m v^{2}, \quad \Gamma \left[ q \right] = \left(t, x, \left( \dfrac{x_{b} - x_{a}}{t_{b}-t_{a}} \right) \right), \quad t_{1} = t_{a}, \quad t_{2} = t_{b},
\end{equation*}

so that

\begin{align*}
S \left[q\right] \left( t_1, t_2 \right) &= \int_{t_{a}}^{t_{b}} \dfrac{m}{2} \left( \dfrac{x_{b} - x_{a}}{t_{b} - t_{a}} \right)^{2} dt \\
&=  \dfrac{m}{2} \left( \dfrac{x_{b} - x_{a}}{t_{b} - t_{a}} \right)^{2} \int_{t_{a}}^{t_{b}} dt \\
&= \dfrac{m}{2} \left( \dfrac{x_{b} - x_{a}}{t_{b} - t_{a}} \right)^{2} \left( t_b - t_{a} \right) = \dfrac{m}{2} \dfrac{\left( x_{b} - x_{a} \right)^{2}}{t_{b} - t_{a}}.
\end{align*}

a. *An ideal planar pendulum consists of a bob of mass* $m$ *connected to a pivot by a massless rod of length* $l$ *subject to uniform gravitational acceleration* $g$. *A Lagrangian is* $L ( t, \theta, \dot{\theta} ) = \frac{1}{2} m l^{2} \dot{\theta}^{2} + mgl \cos \theta$. *The formal parameters of* $L$ *are* $t$, $\theta$, *and* $\dot{\theta}$; $\theta$ *measures the angle of the pendulum rod to a plumb line and* $\dot{\theta}$ *is the angular velocity of the rod.*

A Lagrangian for the specified problem is

\begin{equation*}
L ( t, \theta, \dot{\theta} ) = \frac{1}{2} m l^{2} \dot{\theta}^{2} + mgl \cos \theta.
\end{equation*}

\begin{equation*}
\partial_{1} L ( t, \theta, \dot{\theta} ) = - m g l \sin \theta, \quad \text{and} \quad \partial_{2} L ( t, \theta, \dot{\theta} ) = m l^{2} \dot{\theta}.
\end{equation*}

Now suppose we have a configuration path $q =  \theta$ so that the coordinate tuple at time $t$ is $q(t) =  \theta (t)$. The initial segment of the local tuple at time $t$ is

\begin{equation*}
\Gamma \left[ q \right](t) = \left( t; \theta(t), D \theta (t) \right).
\end{equation*}

The terms of the Lagrange equations are

\begin{equation*}
\left( \partial_{1} L \circ \Gamma \left[ q \right] \right)(t) = - m g l \sin \theta (t),
\end{equation*}

\begin{equation*}
\left( \partial_{2} L \circ \Gamma \left[ q \right] \right)(t) = m l^{2} D \theta (t),
\end{equation*}

\begin{equation*}
D\left( \partial_{2} L \circ \Gamma \left[ q \right] \right)(t) = m l^{2} D^{2} \theta (t),
\end{equation*}

which yield the equation of motion

\begin{equation*}
D^{2} \theta (t) + \left(\dfrac{g}{l}\right) \cos \theta (t) = 0.
\end{equation*}

*A Lagrangian suitable for studying the relative motion of two particles, of masses* $m_{1}$ *and* $m_{2}$, *with potential energy* $V$, *is:*

#+NAME: Lagrangian for central motion
#+begin_src scheme
  (define ((L-central-polar m V) local)
    (let ((q (coordinate local))
          (qdot (velocity local)))
      (let ((r (ref q 0)) (phi (ref q 1))
            (rdot (ref qdot 0)) (phidot (ref qdot 1)))
        (- (* 1/2 m
              (+ (square rdot) (square (* r phidot))) )
           (V r)))))
#+end_src

*The argument* $m$ *is the* /reduced mass/ *of the system*

\begin{equation*}
m = \dfrac{m_{1} m_{2}}{m_{1} + m_{2}}.
\end{equation*}

#+NAME: Gravitational Energy
#+begin_src scheme
  (define ((gravitational-energy G m1 m2) r)

#+end_src

\end{equation*}

and
\begin{equation*}
\partial_{2} L \left( t; r, \phi; v_{r}, v_{\phi} \right) = \left[ m \dot{r}, m \dot{\phi} \right].
\end{equation*}

\begin{equation*}

\end{equation*}

\begin{equation*}

\end{equation*}

\begin{equation*}

\end{equation*}

\begin{equation*}

\end{equation*}

which yield the equations of motion

\begin{equation*}

\end{equation*}

\begin{equation*}

\end{equation*}

It is clear from the second equation that $D \varphi (t)$,the angular frequency $n$, is constant. Rewrite the first equation as

\begin{equation*}
\left(\dfrac{m_{1} m_{2}}{m_{1} + m_{2}}\right) a n^{2} = G \dfrac{m_{1} m_{2}}{a^{2}},
\end{equation*}

and rearrange to furnish the ask

\begin{equation*}
n^{2} a^{3} = G \left( m_{1} + m_{2} \right).
\end{equation*}

\begin{equation*}
\left( \vec{a} \times \vec{b} \right) \cdot \left( \vec{c} \times \vec{d} \right) = \left( \vec{a} \cdot \vec{c} \right) \left( \vec{b} \cdot \vec{d} \right) - \left( \vec{a} \cdot \vec{d} \right) \left( \vec{b} \cdot \vec{c} \right).
\end{equation*}

\begin{align*}
(\xi_{\alpha}^{\bot, \hat{\omega}})^{2} &= \left( \vec{\xi_{\alpha}} - \hat{\omega} \left(\vec{\xi_{\alpha}} \cdot \hat{\omega}\right)  \right)
\cdot
\left( \vec{\xi_{\alpha}} - \hat{\omega} \left(\vec{\xi_{\alpha}} \cdot \hat{\omega}\right)  \right) \\
&= \vec{\xi_{\alpha}} \cdot \vec{\xi_{\alpha}}  - \left( \vec{\xi_{\alpha}} \cdot \hat{\omega} \right) \left( \vec{\xi_{\alpha}} \cdot \hat{\omega} \right) \\

\end{align*}

*Prove that the Euler-Lagrange equations are invariant under the Gauge transformation on the Lagrangian given below. $F(q, t)$ is an arbitrary function.*

\[
   L' = L + \frac{d F(q, t)}{dt}
   \]

Suppose that the mechanical system with the Lagrangian $L'$ undergoes motion such that its generalized coordinates change from $q_{t_{1}}^{(1)}$ at time $t_{1}$ to $q_{t_{2}}^{(2)}$ at time $t_{2}$. The associated action $S'$ is

\begin{align*}
S' = \int_{t_{1}}^{t_{2}} L' dt &= \int_{t_{1}}^{t_{2}} \left ( L + \frac{dF}{dt} \right ) dt\\
&= \int_{t_{1}}^{t_{2}} L dt + \int_{t_{1}}^{t_{2}} \frac{dF}{dt} dt\\
&= \int_{t_{1}}^{t_{2}} L dt + \Big [ F(q, t) \ \Big ]_{t = t_{1}, q=q_{t_{1}}^{(1)}}^{t = t_{2}, q=q_{t_{2}}^{(2)}} \\
&= S + F(q_{t_{2}}^{(2)},t_{2}) - F(q_{t_{1}}^{(1)},t_{1}).
\end{align*}

Because $F(q_{t_{2}}^{(2)},t_{2}) - F(q_{t_{1}}^{(1)},t_{1})$ is independent of $q$ and $\dot{q}$

\[
\delta \left ( F(q_{t_{2}}^{(2)},t_{2}) - F(q_{t_{1}}^{(1)},t_{1}) \right ) = 0.
\]

It follows that

\[
\delta S' = \delta S.
\]

The variations in $S$ and $S'$ being equal, it can only be that the resulting Lagrange equations are the same. $\blacksquare$
