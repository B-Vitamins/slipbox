Consider a [[id:19b3da06-0b11-45c5-a384-dceadc10d934][regression model]] and a [[id:191cd138-08dc-44cd-9b73-ba29e4cda227][classification model]] based on linear combinations of a set of \( M + 1 \) arbitrary [[id:3b151533-7492-4cef-a991-5d314c089f2f][basis functions]] \((\phi_{j})_{j=0}^{M}\):
\begin{align*}
\underbrace{y(\mathbf{x}, \, \mathbf{w})= \sum_{j=0}^{M} w_{j} \cdot \phi_{j}(\mathbf{x})}_{\text{classification}}, \qquad \overbrace{y(\mathbf{x},\, \mathbf{w})=f\left(\sum_{j=0}^{M} w_{j} \cdot \phi_{j}(\mathbf{x})\right)}^{\text{regression}}.
\end{align*}
\(f(\cdot)\) is a nonlinear output activation function. \( \phi_{0} \equiv 1 \) for all \( \mathbf{x} \), is a trivial basis function, merely a preference in notation. It facilitates an interpretation of \( w_{0} \) as a bias parameter. Since \((\phi_{j})_{j=0}^{M}\) is arbitrary, it follows that such models can in principle provide a solution to any regression or classification problem in the trivial sense: we need simply define \(\phi_j \equiv y, \, w_{j} \equiv 1 \), \( (w_{k})_{k \neq j} \equiv 0 \), and define \(f\) to be identity. In other words, if \( y \) was known apriori for all possible values of \( \mathbf{x} \), the very need for basis functions \((\phi_{j})_{j=0}^{M}\) (and the weight vector \( \mathbf{w} \)) vanishes. Models of the form above are only useful in the non-trivial case: when \( y \) is known only for a subset of all possible \( \mathbf{x} \), say \( (\mathbf{x}_{n})_{n=1}^{N} \), called the /training data/. Notwithstanding the fact that given a sufficient number of suitably chosen basis functions, such linear models can approximate any given nonlinear transformation from inputs to outputs to any desired accuracy, the fact that \((\phi_{j})_{j=0}^{M}\) are not functions of \( (\mathbf{x}_{n})_{n=1}^{N} \), i.e., they are independent of the training data, or *fixed*, places limitations on the efficiency with which they can represent input-output mappings. This section explores those limitations.

The *curse of dimensionality* is an umbrella term for the difficulties of building regression and classification models in which the dimensionality of the input variable is large.
#+NAME: Curse of dimensionality in a regression model
#+begin_example
For a \( D \) dimensional input variables \(\mathbf{x} \equiv (x_{i})_{i=1 \ldots D}\), consider a class of /basis functions/ \( (\phi_{i})_{i=1 \ldots D} \), \( (\phi_{ij})_{i,\,j =1 \ldots D}\), \( (\phi_{ijk})_{i,\,j,\,k =1 \ldots D}\), and so on that are defined as polynomials of the components of \( \mathbf{x} \):

\begin{align*}
\phi_{i} (\mathbf{x}) \equiv x_{i}, \quad \phi_{ij} (\mathbf{x}) \equiv x_{i} \cdot x_{j}, \quad \phi_{ijk} (\mathbf{x}) \equiv x_{i} \cdot x_{j} \cdot x_{k}, \, \cdots.
\end{align*}

The associated weights \( (w_{i})_{i=1 \ldots D} \), \( (w_{ij})_{i,\,j =1 \ldots D}\), \( (w_{ijk})_{i,\,j,\,k =1 \ldots D}\), and so on. Clearly, we can define such polynomial basis functions of order \( M \leq D \). For the case \( M = 3 \), we get the following regression model:

\begin{align*}
y(\mathbf{x},\,\mathbf{w})=w_{0}+\sum_{i=1}^{D} w_{i} x_{i}+\sum_{i=1}^{D} \sum_{j=1}^{D} w_{i j} x_{i} x_{j}+\sum_{i=1}^{D} \sum_{j=1}^{D} \sum_{k=1}^{D} w_{i j k} x_{i} x_{j} x_{k}.
\end{align*}

The growth in the number of independent coefficients for a polynomial of order \(M\) is \(\mathcal{O}\left(D^{M}\right)\): that is /exponential in the order/ \( M \) and /polynomial with the dimensionality/ \( D \). 

As \( D \) is increased, a regression model with polynomial basis functions becomes infeasible due to the explosion in the number of independent parameters in the model.
#+end_example
#+NAME: Curse of dimensionality in a classification model
#+begin_example
Consider a set of \(N\) input points \((\mathbf{x}_n)_{n=1 \ldots N}\) of dimension \(D\) with associated class labels \(k_n \in (1, 2, \dots, K)\). Further suppose that \( \mathbf{x} \in I^{D} \subset \mathbb{R}^{D} \) and divide the interval \( I \) into \( S \) sub-intervals. This partitions the input space into \( S^{D} \) grid cells, say \( (V_{j})_{j=1 \ldots S^{D}} \). Consider a set of \( S^{D} \) basis functions defined as

\begin{equation*}
\phi_j(\mathbf{x}) = 
\begin{cases} 
c_j & \text{if } \mathbf{x} \in V_j, \\
0 & \text{otherwise}.
\end{cases}
\end{equation*}

where \( c_{j} \) is the majority class of the training data points in grid cell \( V_{j} \). The associated weights \( (w_{j})_{j=1 \ldots S^{D}} \) are all set to \( 1 \). Assuming that no grid cell is empty, we get the following classification model

\begin{align*}
y(\mathbf{x}, \, \mathbf{w})= \sum_{j=0}^{S^{D}} \phi_{j}(\mathbf{x}).
\end{align*}

The growth in the number of grid cells for \( S \) divisions of the interval \( I \) is \( \mathcal{O} (S^{D}) \): that is /exponential with the dimensionality/ \( D \) and /polynomial with the number of divisions \( S \) of the linear interval/.

As \( D \) is increased, a classification model with a 'majority class' basis functions becomes infeasible due to the explosion in the amount of training data required \( N \sim \mathcal{O} (S^{D}) \) if the critical assumption that no grid cell should be empty is to continue to hold.
#+end_example
+ Circumventing the *curse of dimensionality* requires basis functions that are functions of the input training data, i.e., \( \phi_{j} \equiv f(\mathbf{x}, (\mathbf{x}_{n})_{n=1 \ldots N}) \).
